Using Satellite Imagery to Investigate Rapid Constructions Around Nephrite Mine in eastern Nangarhar Province, Afghanistan

The left satellite image, Figure 1, shows a nephrite mining site in Goshta District of Nangarhar Province from 2019, while the right image, Figure 2, captures the same site in 2023. The rapid constructions visible in the 2023 image illustrate substantial development in the area since the Taliban returned to power in August 2021.

Introduction

The Taliban Islamist group swept to power in Afghanistan in August 2021 following the collapse of the West-backed government under President Mohammad Ashraf Ghani and the chaotic withdrawal of the US forces.

Then, the international community and the US imposed sanctions on the Taliban de facto government as it failed to form an inclusive government and observe human rights, especially women’s rights.

Consequently, Afghanistan lost billions of dollars in humanitarian aid and the Taliban resorted to extracting mines to revive the fragile economy.

Satellite imagery tools, including Google Earth Pro, Google Earth Engine Timelapse and Google Earth Engine Code Editor are of significance for journalists to write investigative stories about legal and illegal mining in countries like Afghanistan where domestic media outlets are forced to do self-censorship.

However, Sentinel Hub EO Browser, which was suggested by (Mossou, 2024) for similar purposes did not work here properly and showed no data for the area from 2019 to 2023.

Background

Global reputable media outlets such as BBC, New York Times and Financial Times reported on how the Taliban had taken over mining resources in Afghanistan.

As (Zucchino & Blanchard, 2025) explained, the Taliban had pinned hopes on the rare earths and other mining resources to breathe life into Afghanistan’s weakening economy.

“The U.S. government estimates that at least $1 trillion in mineral deposits lie beneath Afghanistan’s rugged landscape. The country is rich in copper, gold, zinc, chromite, cobalt, lithium and industrial minerals, as well as in precious and semiprecious gemstones like emeralds, rubies, sapphires, garnets and lapis lazuli (Zucchino & Blanchard, 2025).”

Moreover, Financial Times and BBC have reported on several occasions that since returning to power in Afghanistan, the Taliban group has inked several mining deals with countries like Iran and China which are considered as the US rivals in the region.

China has been seeking to spread its economic influence via its Belt and Road Initiative (BRI).
