Introduction

The Taliban Islamist group swept to power in Afghanistan in August 2021 following the collapse of the West-backed government under President Mohammad Ashraf Ghani and the chaotic withdrawal of the US forces.

Then, the international community and the US imposed sanctions on the Taliban de facto government as it failed to form an inclusive government and observe human rights, especially women’s rights.

Consequently, Afghanistan lost billions of dollars in humanitarian aid and the Taliban resorted to extracting mines to revive the fragile economy.

Satellite imagery tools, including Google Earth Pro, Google Earth Engine Timelapse and Google Earth Engine Code Editor are of significance for journalists to write investigative stories about legal and illegal mining in countries like Afghanistan where domestic media outlets are forced to do self-censorship.

Background

Global reputable media outlets such as BBC, New York Times and Financial Times reported on how the Taliban had taken over mining resources in Afghanistan.

As (Zucchino & Blanchard, 2025) explained, the Taliban had pinned hopes on the rare earths and other mining resources to breathe life into Afghanistan’s weakening economy.

“The U.S. government estimates that at least $1 trillion in mineral deposits lie beneath Afghanistan’s rugged landscape. The country is rich in copper, gold, zinc, chromite, cobalt, lithium and industrial minerals, as well as in precious and semiprecious gemstones like emeralds, rubies, sapphires, garnets and lapis lazuli (Zucchino & Blanchard, 2025).”

Moreover, Financial Times and BBC have reported on several occasions that since returning to power in Afghanistan, the Taliban group has inked several mining deals with countries like Iran and China which are considered as the US rivals in the region.

China has been seeking to spread its economic influence via its Belt and Road Initiative (BRI).

What are bands in Google Earth Engine?

Similar to how a digital camera image captures red, green, and blue data in separate layers, a satellite image consists of layers known as “bands”. Each band holds data from a specific part of the electromagnetic spectrum.

The number, width (i.e., the range of wavelengths a band includes), and distribution of these bands can vary across diaerent satellite datasets, according to a detailed guide.

We observe all the satellite images in what is termed true colours, which duplicates how a scene would appear to the naked eye. However, satellite imaging sensors also generally capture infrared light, which is beyond the scope of normal human vision.

“This is known as ‘multispectral imaging’. This outside-of-visible-range information is particularly useful in understanding the environmental context of a scene, giving information about the geology, vegetation, the presence and quality of water, air quality, crop varieties and even building materials and techniques,” suggest the guide.

“Multispectral images can be useful in investigations as they can highlight changes to the Earth’s surface that wouldn’t be visible to the naked eye, whether due to mining operations, deforestation, or pollution. Most commonly, these images use bands of light from the infrared part of the light spectrum, in addition to those typically visible to the human eye.(Cameron, 2025)”

Conclusion

This investigation showed the importance of satellite imagery as a powerful tool for analysing and investigating developments such as rapid constructions and activities around a nephrite mine in Taliban-controlled Afghanistan.

Throughout this investigation, it became clear from 2019 and 2023 satellite images, alongside with the data from the Google Earth Engine, that there is a rise in rapid constructions and mine extractions in the area.

Moreover, this project highlighted the importance of how free satellite tools can independently verify developments on the ground, especially in war or censorship countries.

The investigation contributes to investigative journalism and highlighted the vital role of open-source intelligence to hold authoritarian governments such as the Taliban accountable in countries where media outlets are forced to do self-censorship to help maintain transparency and professionalism in mining extractions.
